Theorem 5.3.5. Zero Derivative Theorem.

Let \(f(x)\) be differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) If \(f^{\prime}(x) = 0\) for all \(x\) in \((a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f(x)\) has to be a constant.

Proof.

The idea of the proof is to use Theorem 5.3.3 to show that \(f(x_{2}) - f(x_{1}) = 0\) for any points \(x_{1},x_{2}\) in \((a,b)\text{.}\) Now, by Theorem 5.3.3 we have
\begin{equation*} f(x_{2}) - f(x_{1}) = f^{\prime}(c)(x_{2}-x_{1}) \end{equation*}
for some \(c\) in \((a,b)\text{.}\) However, \(f^{\prime}(c) = 0\) by assumption, and so \(f(x_{2}) - f(x_{1}) = 0\text{.}\) In other words, \(f(x_{2}) = f(x_{1})\text{.}\) Hence \(f\) must be constant.
