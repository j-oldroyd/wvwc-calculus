Example 9.7.9. Taylor series for \(e^{-x^2}\).

Assuming that \(e^{x}\) equals its Taylor series, find the Maclaurin series for \(e^{-x^{2}}\text{.}\) Also, find the \(70^{\text{th}}\) derivative of \(e^{-x^{2}}\) at \(x = 0\text{.}\)
Solution.
We could use Definition 9.7.7 to find the Maclaurin series for \(e^{-x^{2}}\text{,}\) but it’s far, far easier to use the series for \(e^{x}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} e^{-x^{2}} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-x^{2})^{k}}{k!} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}(-1)^{k}\frac{x^{2k}}{k!}\text{.} \end{equation*}
It turns out that this also lets us find derivatives at \(0\) incredibly quickly. If we let \(f(x) = e^{-x^{2}}\text{,}\) then it follows that \(\frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!}\) is the coefficient of \(x^{k}\) in the power series for \(f(x)\text{.}\) Therefore
\begin{equation*} \frac{f^{(70)}(0)}{70!} = \frac{(-1)^{35}}{35!} \Rightarrow f^{(70)}(0) = -\frac{70!}{35!}\text{.} \end{equation*}
