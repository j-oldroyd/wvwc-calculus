Example 9.4.3. Alternating Harmonic Series.

Show that the Alternating Harmonic Series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k + 1}}{k}\) converges, and determine a value of \(n\) for which \(S_{n}\) is within \(.001\) of the actual value of \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k}\text{.}\)

Solution.
Since the Alternating Harmonic Series is an alternating series with \(a_{k} = \frac{1}{k}\text{,}\) and because these terms decrease to \(0\text{,}\) the sum must converge. However, we do not yet know what it converges to yet. Now let \(S_{n}\) denote the \(n^{\text{th}}\) partial sum. Then we know the error between \(S\) and \(S_{n}\) is at most
\begin{equation*} |a_{n+1}| = \frac{1}{n + 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
To make this less than \(.001\text{,}\) we must have \(n\geq 999\text{.}\)
