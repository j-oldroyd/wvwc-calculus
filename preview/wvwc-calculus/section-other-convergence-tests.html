    Section 9.4 Other Convergence Tests

    Subsection Alternating Series

    An alternating series is any series whose terms switch sign. Written in summation notation, they take the form \(\sum (-1)^{k}a_{k}\) where \(a_{k}\) is a positive sequence. Alternating series have a very useful test for convergence.

    Theorem 9.4.1. Alternating Series Test.

    Consider the alternating series \(S = \sum (-1)^{k}a_{k}\text{,}\) where \(a_{k}\) is positive and decreasing. If \(a_{k}\to0\text{,}\) then the series converges. Furthermore, for such a series we have the remainder estimate
    \begin{equation*} |R_{n}| = |S - S_{n}| \leq |a_{n+1}|\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that Theorem 9.4.1 is not the same as Theorem 9.3.1.

    Example 9.4.2. An Alternating Series with Roots.

    Does
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n = 6}^{\infty}(-1)^{n}\frac{n^{\frac{n + 1}{n}} - n}{n} \end{equation*}
    converge or diverge?
    Solution.
    Let \(a_{n} = \frac{n^{\frac{n+1}{n}} - n}{n} = n^{1/n} - 1\text{.}\) Then \(a_{n}\) is decreasing, and \(a_{n}\to0\text{,}\) so the series converges.

    Example 9.4.3. Alternating Harmonic Series.

    Show that the Alternating Harmonic Series \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k + 1}}{k}\) converges, and determine a value of \(n\) for which \(S_{n}\) is within \(.001\) of the actual value of \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since the Alternating Harmonic Series is an alternating series with \(a_{k} = \frac{1}{k}\text{,}\) and because these terms decrease to \(0\text{,}\) the sum must converge. However, we do not yet know what it converges to yet. Now let \(S_{n}\) denote the \(n^{\text{th}}\) partial sum. Then we know the error between \(S\) and \(S_{n}\) is at most
    \begin{equation*} |a_{n+1}| = \frac{1}{n + 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To make this less than \(.001\text{,}\) we must have \(n\geq 999\text{.}\)
    The Alternating Harmonic Series is also a useful example to illustrate the following definitions.

    Definition 9.4.4. Absolute and Conditional Convergence.

    A series \(\sum a_{k}\) is absolutely convergent if \(\sum |a_{k}|\) converges. A series \(\sum a_{k}\) is conditionally convergent if it converges but \(\sum|a_{k}|\) diverges.
    The Alternating Harmonic Series is an example of a conditionally convergent series. There are two important consequences of Definition 9.4.4:
    1. Absolutely convergent series are also convergent series.
    2. For conditionally convergent series, order matters.

    Example 9.4.5. Convergence of a Series Involving Sine.

    Determine if
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=4}^{\infty}\frac{\sin(3n - \sqrt{n})}{3^{n}} \end{equation*}
    converges or diverges.
    Solution.
    If we take the absolute value of each term, then we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{|\sin(3n - \sqrt{n})|}{3^{n}} \leq \frac{1}{3^{n}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\sum_{n=4}^{\infty}3^{-n}\) is a geometric series with \(|r|\lt 1\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=4}^{\infty}\left|\frac{|\sin(3n - \sqrt{n})|}{3^{n}}\right| \end{equation*}
    must converge by Theorem 9.3.9.
    Hence the original series is absolutely convergent, and so also convergent.

    Subsection Ratio Test

    Geometric series are among the easiest to sum and determine convergence for. So it’s useful to try to compare an arbitrary series with a geometric series. The main idea is to look at the long-term behavior of ratios of consecutive terms.

    Theorem 9.4.6. Ratio Test.

    Let \(\sum a_{k}\) be an infinite series and let
    \begin{equation*} r = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_{k}}\right|\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then
    1. If \(r \lt 1\) the series converges absolutely.
    2. If \(r \gt 1\) the series diverges.
    3. If \(r = 1\) the test fails.

    Example 9.4.7. Using the Ratio Test.

    Does
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k-1}3^{2k+1}}{k^{2}5^{k}} \end{equation*}
    converge or diverge?
    Solution.
    Since
    \begin{equation*} r = \frac{9}{5} \gt 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    the series diverges by Theorem 9.4.6.
    The ratio test works well with series whose terms involve factorials or powers involving \(k\text{.}\)

    Example 9.4.8. Factorials over Powers.

    Show that \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{k!}{k^{k}}\) converges.
    Solution.
    Since \(a_{k} = \frac{k!}{k^{k}}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{k\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_{k}}\right| = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left(\frac{k}{k+1}\right)^{k}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can find this limit using L’Hospital’s Rule (see Section 5.3) since this limit is the indeterminate form \([1^{\infty}]\text{.}\) So set \(y = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left(\frac{k}{k+1}\right)^{k}\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \ln y \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}k\ln\left(\frac{k}{k+1}\right) \\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{\ln k - \ln(k+1)}{1/k} \\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{1/k - 1/(k+1)}{-1/k^{2}} \\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{-k^{2}}{k(k+1)} \\ \amp = -1 \text{.} \end{align*}
    Therefore \(y = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left(\frac{k}{k+1}\right)^{k} = e^{-1}\text{,}\) which means that the series converges by the ratio test.

    Subsection Root Test

    The root test is similar to the ratio test in that it compares a given series with an appropriate geometric series to determine if the original converges.

    Theorem 9.4.9. Root Test.

    Let \(\sum a_{k}\) be an infinite series and let
    \begin{equation*} r = \lim_{k\to\infty}\sqrt[k]{|a_{k}|}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then
    1. If \(r \lt 1\) the series converges absolutely.
    2. If \(r \gt 1\) the series diverges.
    3. If \(r = 1\) the test fails.
